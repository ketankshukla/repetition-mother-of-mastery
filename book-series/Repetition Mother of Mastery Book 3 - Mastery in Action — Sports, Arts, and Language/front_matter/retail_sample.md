I was sitting in the stands at a gymnastics competition when I saw something that changed how I understood mastery forever.

A young gymnast — she could not have been more than sixteen — stepped onto the balance beam. The beam is four inches wide. Four inches. The width of a smartphone. And on this impossibly narrow strip of wood, four feet above the ground, she proceeded to execute a routine that defied everything I thought I knew about the limits of human ability.

She leaped. She spun. She flipped backward through the air and landed — on four inches of wood — with the precision of a surgeon and the grace of a dancer. Her body moved with a fluidity that seemed to violate the laws of physics.

The audience gasped. They applauded. And the word I heard whispered around me was the same word I had heard my entire life when watching extraordinary performance: talent.

But I knew better.

I knew that this sixteen-year-old had been training since she was four. She had spent twelve years — more than seventy-five percent of her entire life — repeating the same movements thousands upon thousands of times.

I knew that what the audience was calling talent was actually myelin — layers upon layers of it, wrapped around the neural pathways that controlled her balance, her spatial awareness, her muscle coordination. Her brain had been physically restructured by repetition.

And I knew that the same process was at work in every domain where human beings achieve extraordinary performance.
