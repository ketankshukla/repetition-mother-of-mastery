I met a man once who had mastered everything he touched.

He was not famous. He was not wealthy by conventional standards. He did not have a social media following or a bestselling book. He was a carpenter — a man who built furniture with his hands, in a small workshop behind his house.

But his furniture was extraordinary. Every joint was perfect. Every surface was flawless. Every piece had a quality of presence — a solidity, a rightness, a beauty — that made you want to touch it, to sit in it, to keep it forever.

I asked him how he had achieved this level of mastery.

He smiled. "I have been doing this for forty years. Every day. The same tools. The same wood. The same joints. I have made the same cuts so many times that my hands know what to do before my mind tells them. There is no secret. There is only the work. Every day. For forty years."

I pressed him. Surely there was more to it than that. A special technique. A mentor who taught him secrets. A natural gift.

He shook his head. "The work is the secret. The repetition is the gift. And the mentor was time — forty years of showing up and doing the work, day after day, until the work became part of me and I became part of the work."
