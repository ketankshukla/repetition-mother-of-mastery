I spent years practicing the wrong way.

Not mindlessly — I understood deliberate practice. Not inconsistently — I had built the habits. Not without passion — I loved what I was doing. But I was practicing with the wrong techniques. I was using methods that felt productive but were scientifically proven to be inefficient.

The moment everything changed was the moment I discovered active recall.

I had been studying for a professional certification. My approach was the same one I had used throughout my education: read the material, highlight the important parts, reread the highlights, and repeat until the information felt familiar.

It was the approach that virtually everyone uses. And it was, according to the research, one of the least effective learning strategies in existence.

A colleague who had passed the same certification on their first attempt shared their secret. They did not reread. They did not highlight. Instead, they tested themselves. Relentlessly. They would read a section once, close the book, and try to recall everything they had just read.

I was skeptical. Testing yourself seemed like a way to measure learning, not a way to produce it.

But the research was unambiguous: active recall produces two to three times more retention than rereading or any other passive review strategy.

I switched my approach. Within two weeks, I could feel the difference. Information that had been slippery was now solid, accessible, and reliable.
